### UVP-01: POPIA Section 21 Written Operator Agreement

**Classification:** Flagship UVP — lead with this in all medical practice communications

**The regulatory basis**

POPIA Section 21(1) requires every responsible party (the medical practice) to have a written contract with any operator (third party) that processes personal information on their behalf. The contract must confirm the operator maintains the security measures set out in Section 19.

Section 19 requires "appropriate, reasonable, technical and organisational measures" to secure the integrity and confidentiality of personal information. For a medical practice handling health data (special personal information under Section 26), this is a high bar.

**The enforcement precedent**

In September 2023, the Information Regulator issued an Enforcement Notice to a South African pharmaceutical company after a third-party IT provider suffered a brute force cyber attack. Approximately 3.6 million data subjects' records were compromised. The pharmaceutical company was held responsible because it had failed to have proper operator contracts in place with its provider. The breach happened to the provider; the Enforcement Notice went to the company.

Failure to comply with an Enforcement Notice is a criminal offence: fines up to R 10 million, imprisonment up to 10 years, or both. In July 2023, the Information Regulator imposed a R 5 million fine on the Department of Justice for failing to comply with an Enforcement Notice.

Source: Fasken, September 2023

**Plain language articulation**

> Your practice handles patient health information every day. Under South African law, you're required to have a signed, written agreement with any IT company that touches, stores, backs up, or has access to that data. That agreement must confirm they maintain specific security measures to protect patient information. Without it, your practice carries the full regulatory risk if anything goes wrong, even if the breach happens on your IT provider's side.

**The three questions (use in every medical prospect conversation)**

1. Do you have a signed written agreement with your current IT provider that specifically addresses POPIA Section 21? Not a general service agreement. A written contract that names the practice as responsible party, names the IT provider as operator, and sets out the security measures they maintain.

2. Does that agreement satisfy the new health data regulations (Gazette 54268, March 2026)? A contract signed before March 2026 almost certainly doesn't address these.

3. Is your IT provider a specialist in medical practice IT, or do they also fix printers at accounting firms? The new regulations require security measures aligned with generally accepted practices for your sector.

**What ZA Support provides**

ZA Support's service agreement is a POPIA Section 21 compliant operator agreement. It names the practice as responsible party and ZA Support as operator; sets out the Section 19 security measures maintained; addresses breach notification, confidentiality, data retention, destruction, and return on termination; and is drafted for the medical practice context.

**Verification**

| Claim | How to verify |
|---|---|
| Signed agreement exists | Contract on file with date |
| Agreement addresses Section 21 | Contract contains Section 19 measures, breach notification, confidentiality terms |
| Agreement reflects March 2026 regulations | Contract version or addendum dated after 06/03/2026 |
| Security measures are maintained | Health Check data, incident logs, backup verification records |

### UVP-05: Practitioner Personal Liability Awareness

**The regulatory basis**

Under HPCSA rules, practitioners are personally liable for the security of patient records. Unencrypted devices containing patient data create professional conduct risk. Under POPIA, the responsible party (the practice, and by extension the information officer, which defaults to the CEO/principal practitioner) carries liability for compliance.

The National Health Act Section 14 places confidentiality obligations on all practice staff, not just the doctor. Section 15 limits disclosure to consent or court order.

**Plain language articulation**

> The compliance obligations don't sit with your IT company. They sit with you. As the principal practitioner, you're personally liable for the security of patient records under HPCSA rules. Under POPIA, the information officer of the practice (which is you, unless you've formally appointed someone else) is responsible for compliance. Having the right IT partner doesn't transfer your liability. It helps you meet it.

**Why this matters commercially**

This UVP reframes the buying decision. The doctor isn't choosing between IT providers. They're choosing how to meet a personal regulatory obligation. ZA Support becomes the mechanism for meeting that obligation, not just a service provider.

### UVP-06: Seven-Category Risk Scoring Framework

**The factual basis**

ZA Support assesses every medical practice across seven specific risk categories, each scored from Critical to Low:

1. Network Security (WPA/weak passwords/no firewall through to WPA3-Enterprise, enterprise firewall, full segmentation)
2. Email Security (basic ISP email/no MFA through to enterprise gateway, auto-encryption, advanced threat protection)

5. Device Security (no antivirus/no encryption/no lock through to enterprise AV, full disk encryption, MDM, cable locks)
6. Physical Security (open access/devices visible to patients through to access logs, privacy screens, cable locks, secure disposal)
7. Monitoring and Response (no monitoring/no plans through to 24/7 SOC, tested incident plans, less than 15-minute response)

**Plain language articulation**

> We don't give you a vague "your IT is fine" or "your IT needs work." We score your practice across seven specific areas that affect patient data security. Each area gets a clear rating. You see exactly where you're strong and exactly where the gaps are. The report tells you what needs fixing, in what order, and why it matters for your patients and your compliance.

### UVP-07: Gazette 54268 — March 2026 Health Data Regulations

**The regulatory basis**

On 6 March 2026, the Information Regulator published Government Gazette No. 54268: "Regulations relating to the Processing of Data Subjects' Health Information by Certain Responsible Parties, 2026." These regulations came into force immediately with no grace period.

They apply to healthcare providers, medical schemes, insurers, employers, pension funds, and their operators. They require: technical and organisational safeguards aligned with generally accepted information security practices for the specific sector; processing under a documented duty of confidentiality; measures addressing both physical and electronic records; proper disposal procedures to prevent unauthorised access after disposal.

**Plain language articulation**

> New regulations published on 6 March 2026 specifically govern how health information must be handled. They came into force the same day with no grace period. If your practice's IT setup and your agreement with your IT provider haven't been reviewed since then, there's a gap. These aren't general data protection rules. They're specific to health information and they add requirements on top of what POPIA already demands.

**Why this is time-sensitive**

Any service agreement, operator contract, or compliance framework created before 6 March 2026 almost certainly doesn't address these regulations. This creates an immediate review obligation for every medical practice and an immediate commercial opportunity for ZA Support: reach out to existing clients to confirm compliance, and use the regulatory update as a conversation starter with prospects.

### UVP-08: Predictive Maintenance (Failure Prevention, Not Failure Response)

**The factual basis**

Health Check's trending engine analyses device metrics over time and flags potential failures 7 to 14 days before they occur. This means a hard drive approaching failure, a battery degrading below safe thresholds, or a security certificate about to expire triggers an alert and action before the practice experiences downtime or data loss.

**Plain language articulation**

> We don't wait for something to break. Health Check watches every device in your practice and spots problems developing before they cause disruption. A hard drive showing early signs of failure gets flagged and replaced before you lose patient data. That's the difference between reactive IT (fixing things after they break) and predictive IT (preventing them from breaking in the first place).

**Why this matters for medical practices specifically**

Downtime in a medical practice doesn't just mean inconvenience. It means patients can't be seen, billing stops, records become inaccessible, and if the failure involves data loss, there's a potential POPIA breach to report. Predictive maintenance prevents these cascading consequences.

### UVP-11: Breach Notification Readiness (72-Hour Compliance)

**The regulatory basis**

POPIA Section 22 requires notification to the Information Regulator and affected data subjects when there are reasonable grounds to believe personal information has been accessed by an unauthorised person. Section 21(2) requires the operator (ZA Support) to notify the responsible party (the practice) immediately when a breach is suspected.

The notification must include sufficient information about the breach, the nature of the data affected, recommended steps, and the measures taken to address the breach. The responsible party must notify the Information Regulator "as soon as reasonably possible" after discovery. Industry practice treats this as a 72-hour window.

**Plain language articulation**

> If something goes wrong, the clock starts immediately. You have to notify the Information Regulator and your affected patients promptly. Most practices don't have a breach notification process in place. They wouldn't know where to start. ZA Support's service includes breach detection, immediate notification to your practice, documentation of what happened, and support through the notification process. We don't just fix the technical problem. We help you meet the regulatory obligation.

**What ZA Support provides**

Continuous monitoring for unauthorised access indicators; immediate notification to the practice when a potential breach is detected; documentation of the incident with timestamps; support for drafting the Information Regulator notification; support for drafting data subject notifications; evidence preservation for potential regulatory inquiry.

**The regulatory basis**

POPIA operator agreements must address what happens to personal information when the relationship ends. The operator must either securely delete or return all personal information to the responsible party and provide certification of destruction.

**Plain language articulation**

> If you ever move to a different IT provider, your patient data doesn't disappear into a black hole. Practices that understand their data handling obligations under POPIA are less likely to churn casually. Switching IT providers means managing a data return/destruction process, re-establishing a new operator agreement with the next provider, and losing the documented compliance history built up with ZA Support.

5. **Medical aid data flow coverage**: Billing and claims processing involves medical scheme data, expanding the operator agreement scope into financial and health data simultaneously.
