## Self-monitoring / verification techniques (cited)

**Headline empirical finding across the field:** self-monitoring that relies on *the same model grading its own reasoning with no external signal* is largely unreliable and can make outputs worse. Verification that introduces an **independent or ground-truth-grounded signal** (a test, a separate verifier, an environment outcome) is what actually reduces silent failure.

### What works (evidence-backed)

- **Self-consistency** — sample many independent reasoning paths, return the **majority-vote answer**. Wang, Wei, Schuurmans, Le, Chi, Narang, Chowdhery, Zhou (Google), *"Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models"*, arXiv:2203.11171, Mar 2022 (ICLR 2023). https://arxiv.org/abs/2203.11171 — Reported gains on PaLM-540B: GSM8K 56.5%→74.4%, SVAMP 79.0%→86.6%, AQuA 35.8%→48.3%. A genuine but weak signal: it only helps where a checkable final answer is extractable, and **cannot fix a systematically-wrong model** (the majority is then also wrong).

- **Reflexion** — agent converts an *external* task-feedback signal (unit tests, environment reward) into a verbal self-reflection stored in episodic memory, then retries. Shinn, Cassano, Berman, Gopinath, Narasimhan, Yao, *"Reflexion: Language Agents with Verbal Reinforcement Learning"*, arXiv:2303.11366, NeurIPS 2023. https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.11366 · code https://github.com/noahshinn/reflexion — 91% pass@1 on HumanEval. **Crucial:** the *trigger* is an external outcome signal, not introspection. It works because it closes the loop on a real signal.

- **Separate verifier model / generator–verifier gap** — train a *distinct* verifier to score candidate solutions and select the best. Cobbe et al. (OpenAI), *"Training Verifiers to Solve Math Word Problems"*, arXiv:2110.14168, Oct 2021 (introduced GSM8K). https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.14168 — Verification outperformed pure fine-tuning and scaled better with data (roughly equivalent to a 30× model-size increase on GSM8K). Judging correctness is a different, often easier, task than producing the answer. ("Generator–verifier gap" as a single canonical citation is a cross-paper framing, not one named paper — **[UNVERIFIED]** as a single source.)

- **Process reward (supervise the steps, not just the answer)** — Lightman et al. (OpenAI), *"Let's Verify Step by Step"*, arXiv:2305.20050, May 2023 (ICLR 2024); released PRM800K. https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.20050 — Process supervision (reward each reasoning step) **significantly outperforms outcome supervision** for selecting correct solutions; their PRM solved 78% of a representative MATH subset via best-of-N. Outcome-only checking lets a wrong-process/right-answer (or unverifiable) trajectory pass; step-level scoring catches and localises the defect. Automatic-label variants: Wang et al., *"Math-Shepherd"*, arXiv:2312.08935, ACL 2024 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.08935); Luo et al. (DeepMind), *"...Automated Process Supervision"* (OmegaPRM), arXiv:2406.06592, Jun 2024 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.06592).

### What is theatre (evidence-backed — do NOT trust as a gate)

- **Intrinsic self-correction of reasoning fails.** Huang et al. (Google DeepMind/Research), *"Large Language Models Cannot Self-Correct Reasoning Yet"*, arXiv:2310.01798, ICLR 2024. https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.01798 — With **no external feedback and no oracle label**, LLMs **do not improve and frequently degrade** performance after self-correcting (GSM8K, CommonSenseQA, HotpotQA). Prior reported gains often relied on oracle labels to decide *when to stop*. This is the single most important result for designing agent self-checks: a self-critique loop with no ground-truth signal can convert correct answers to incorrect ones.

- **The critique carries little information.** Stechly, Marquez, Kambhampati, *"GPT-4 Doesn't Know It's Wrong..."*, arXiv:2310.12397, Oct 2023 (NeurIPS 2023 FMDM workshop). https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.12397 — On graph colouring, GPT-4 is no better at *verifying* than *producing*; LLM-critiquing-LLM loops do not help, and the *content* of the critique is largely irrelevant to whether iterative prompting improves the answer. Sound **external** verifiers did help.

- **Self-Refine gains are concentrated on subjective tasks.** Madaan et al., *"Self-Refine: Iterative Refinement with Self-Feedback"*, arXiv:2303.17651, NeurIPS 2023. https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.17651 — ~20% average absolute improvement, but strongest on open-ended/stylistic tasks where "better" is loosely defined; weak or harmful on hard right/wrong tasks. Easy to over-generalise into theatre on correctness-critical steps.

- **LLM-as-judge is biased and not a per-output correctness gate.** Zheng et al., *"Judging LLM-as-a-Judge with MT-Bench and Chatbot Arena"*, arXiv:2306.05685, NeurIPS 2023. https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.05685 — A strong judge reaches >80% agreement with humans (good as a coarse *preference* signal), but has documented **position bias, verbosity bias** (a "repetitive list" attack fools it), and **self-enhancement bias**. As an agent's self-pass gate it can stamp "verified" on verbose-but-wrong output.

**Design rule the literature supports:** a self-monitoring step is only as trustworthy as the *independence and ground-truth-grounding of its signal*. A gate that lets the generator re-read and approve its own work, with no external check, is **non-evidence** — exactly the false-"verified" status that produces silent failure.

## Goal-alignment & comprehension techniques (cited)

**The core problem named in the literature:** an agent can *succeed mechanically while missing the real goal* — either by gaming a flawed proxy (specification gaming) or by competently pursuing the wrong objective even under a correct spec (goal misgeneralization).

- **Ground actions against external state instead of trusting internal reasoning.** Yao et al., *"ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models"*, arXiv:2210.03629, 2022 (ICLR 2023). https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.03629 — Interleaving reasoning with actions+observations lets the model "induce, track, and update action plans as well as handle exceptions"; grounding against an external API reduces the hallucination/error-propagation seen in reasoning-only chains. This is the canonical "verify against the world, not your own narrative" pattern.

- **Plan-then-act / decompose before executing.** Wang et al., *"Plan-and-Solve Prompting..."*, ACL 2023. https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-long.147/ — Devise a plan and subtasks *first*, then execute, to reduce missing-step errors; became the "Plan-and-Execute" agent pattern (separate planner from executor).

- **Spec-driven / acceptance-criteria-driven development = the externalised "definition of done."** Thoughtworks, *"Spec-driven development..."*, 2025. https://www.thoughtworks.com/en-us/insights/blog/agile-engineering-practices/spec-driven-development-unpacking-2025-new-engineering-practices — A versioned, structured specification becomes the source of truth; it directly targets the 2024–25 agent failure modes of **intent drift, unverifiable output (no acceptance criteria = no way to know the agent's work is right), and endless review.** Microsoft for Developers, *"Spec-Driven Development: A Spec-First Approach to AI-Native Engineering"*, 2025: the agent reads the spec → plans → decomposes → builds → **verifies the result against the original acceptance criteria**; the spec is durable, the prompt is disposable. https://developer.microsoft.com/blog/spec-driven-development-ai-native-engineering

- **Anthropic, "Building Effective Agents", 19 Dec 2024.** https://www.anthropic.com/research/building-effective-agents — Use the simplest pattern that works; distinguish *workflows* (predefined code paths) from *agents* (self-directed). Names the **evaluator-optimizer loop** (one LLM generates, a *separate* one grades against criteria and feeds back) as an explicit acceptance-criteria check. Warns that frameworks add abstraction that "obscure[s] the underlying prompts and responses, making them harder to debug."

- **Reward hacking / specification gaming — why a mechanically-passing run misses intent.** Amodei, Olah, Steinhardt, Christiano, Schulman, Mané, *"Concrete Problems in AI Safety"*, arXiv:1606.06565, 2016. https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.06565 — "Reward hacking": a solution that formally maximises the objective but "perverts the spirit of the designer's intent", caused by partially-observed/imperfect goal proxies and complexity that is hackable. DeepMind, Krakovna et al., *"Specification gaming: the flip side of AI ingenuity"*, 2020. https://deepmind.google/blog/specification-gaming-the-flip-side-of-ai-ingenuity/ — behaviour "that satisfies the literal specification of an objective without achieving the intended outcome." Running examples list: https://vkrakovna.wordpress.com/2018/04/02/specification-gaming-examples-in-ai/

- **Goal misgeneralization — wrong goal even with a CORRECT spec.** Langosco et al., *"Goal Misgeneralization in Deep Reinforcement Learning"*, ICML 2022, arXiv:2105.14111 (https://proceedings.mlr.press/v162/langosco22a.html) — an agent can keep its *capabilities* out-of-distribution yet pursue the *wrong goal*. Shah et al. (DeepMind), *"Goal Misgeneralization: Why Correct Specifications Aren't Enough For Correct Goals"*, arXiv:2210.01790, Oct 2022 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.01790) — a system can pursue an undesired goal *even when the reward/spec is correct*. (These two papers are distinct and often conflated.)

**Relevance to the failing pipeline:** "publish 8 posts/day" is a proxy. An agent optimising the proxy can satisfy it mechanically (files generated, exit 0) while missing the real goal (net-new *live, indexed, non-doorway* posts). The fix the literature points to is an externalised acceptance criterion graded against real world-state, not the agent's own report.

## Agent failure-mode taxonomy (cited)

- **MAST — the empirical multi-agent failure taxonomy.** Cemri, Pan, Yang, et al. (UC Berkeley et al.), *"Why Do Multi-Agent LLM Systems Fail?"*, arXiv:2503.13657, 2025 (NeurIPS 2025 Datasets & Benchmarks). https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.13657 — Built from 7 frameworks, 200+ tasks, 1,600+ annotated traces, Cohen's Kappa 0.88. **14 failure modes in 3 categories: (i) specification issues** (poor task/role spec, missing termination logic), **(ii) inter-agent misalignment** (communication breakdowns, withheld information), **(iii) task verification** (inadequate verification and termination checks). Re-verified during compilation: *step repetition alone caused ~17% of failures because agents lacked awareness of prior progress/completion status* — a direct analogue of the pipeline regenerating posts it never landed. Central finding: failures are **not** mostly the base model's fault — the same model single-agent often beats the multi-agent version; the lever is **architecture** (clear roles, cross-verification, reliable termination).

- **Error compounding — the maths of cascade failure.** Patel et al., *"The Six Sigma Agent: Achieving Enterprise-Grade Reliability in LLM Systems..."*, arXiv:2601.22290, 29 Jan 2026 (recent preprint; re-verified to exist). https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.22290 — Even **99% per-step accuracy degrades to 36.6% over 100 steps** (and far worse beyond): high-accuracy models "fail catastrophically in multi-step workflows due to error compounding." Proposed antidote is redundancy + consensus voting across independent samples (system error ~O(p^⌈n/2⌉)). The figure (re-confirmed) matters because a long generate→sanitise→commit→deploy→index chain multiplies per-step failure.

- **Silent error propagation / no negative-feedback loop.** A subtly-wrong intermediate output "passes through intact, and every downstream agent treats it as fact" (the MAST line of work; restatement in the Six Sigma preprint, https://arxiv.org/html/2601.22290). This is the silent-failure mechanism: nothing in the chain is empowered to say "stop, this is wrong."

- **Context loss / lost-in-the-middle.** Liu et al. (Stanford et al.), *"Lost in the Middle: How Language Models Use Long Contexts"*, TACL 2023, arXiv:2307.03172. https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.03172 — Performance is highest when relevant info is at the **start or end** of context and degrades when it must be retrieved from the **middle**, and drops as context grows. A rule buried mid-prompt (e.g. "never publish a doorway page", "respect the velocity cap") is exactly where it is least reliably applied — a plausible contributor to "no matter what is programmed, it recurs."

- **Persistent cross-architecture failure modes.** *"Survey on Evaluation of LLM-based Agents"*, arXiv:2503.16416, 2025. https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.16416 — common modes **looping, context loss, tool misuse** "persist across architectures"; evaluations still lack robustness/adversarial tests. *"Evaluation and Benchmarking of LLM Agents: A Survey"*, arXiv:2507.21504, 2025 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.21504) — reliability must probe **worst-case** as well as average-case, because average success hides goal-missing failures.

- **AutoGPT's documented planning failures (the negative template).** vectara/awesome-agent-failures case study. https://github.com/vectara/awesome-agent-failures/blob/main/docs/case-studies/autogpt-planning-failures.md — (1) **No objective "done"**: relied on natural-language self-assessment of completion, which defaulted to "more work needed"; (2) **loop without progress memory**: regenerated near-identical plans without recognising prior attempts; (3) **goal drift**. These are the same three pathologies the pipeline exhibits.

**Recurring failure classes, named:** specification/termination gaps · inadequate task-verification · silent error propagation (no negative feedback) · error cascade/compounding · context loss (lost-in-the-middle) · looping without progress memory · reward hacking / spec gaming · goal misgeneralization · self-assessed (LLM-judged) completion.

## Negative-control / falsification practice

**Principle:** a test that *cannot fail* proves nothing. You must demonstrate a test's discriminative power — that it returns a FAILING verdict on a known-bad input — before trusting its PASS on the real input. (This is the formal-engineering articulation of falsifiability; no single "Popper-on-testing" primary source is cited here — the principle is established through the concrete practices below — **[UNVERIFIED]** as a named Popper-on-software citation.)

- **Mutation testing — "test your tests" by injecting faults.** Deliberately mutate the code; if the suite still passes, the mutant "survived" and exposes a weak/missing assertion. PIT (PITest) is the canonical JVM tool; "test strength" = killed ÷ total mutants in covered code, isolating tests that *execute* a line but *assert nothing*. *"Test Your Tests: Mutation Testing in Java with PIT"*, JAVAPRO, Jan 2026 (https://javapro.io/2026/01/21/test-your-tests-mutation-testing-in-java-with-pit/); *Simple Mutation Testing with PIT*, MECO Engineering, 2026 (https://medium.com/meco-engineering/simple-mutation-testing-with-pit-f9ffbcf16cbe). Python peers: **mutmut / cosmic-ray** — **[UNVERIFIED]** (docs not fetched). Code coverage alone is a weak signal; mutation score is stronger.

- **Negative controls in ML evaluation — randomization/sanity baselines.** Adebayo, Gilmer, Muelly, Goodfellow, Hardt, Kim, *"Sanity Checks for Saliency Maps"*, NeurIPS 2018, arXiv:1810.03292. https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.03292 — Two negative controls: compare the method's output on a *trained* model vs a *randomly-initialised* model, and vs a model trained on *permuted labels*. If the output looks the same in the scrambled case, the method is measuring noise. Several popular methods **failed** these checks. The transferable lesson: if your evaluation passes even on scrambled input, the evaluation is worthless.

- **Metamorphic testing — for systems with NO oracle** (you cannot enumerate the "correct article"). Chen, Cheung, Yiu (1998); survey: Segura et al., *"A Survey on Metamorphic Testing"*, IEEE TSE 2016, and ACM Computing Surveys 51(1), 2018 (https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3143561). Instead of an exact oracle, assert **metamorphic relations** that must hold between related inputs/outputs — e.g. for the pipeline: *a doorway-flagged input must never publish*; *scrambling the keyword must change the output*; *the same brief twice must not yield byte-identical prose*. These are falsifiable relations a generative pipeline can be held to.

- **Flaky tests / non-determinism mask falsifiability.** Luo, Hariri, Eloussi, Marinov, *"An Empirical Analysis of Flaky Tests"*, FSE 2014 (DOI 10.1145/2635868.2635920) — top causes: async wait (~45%), concurrency (~20%), test-order dependency (~12%). Python-specific: order-dependency ~59% (Gruber et al., arXiv:2101.09077). At Google, >80% of test-output transitions are flakiness, costing 2–16% of CI resources (cited in *Systemic Flakiness*, EASE 2025, https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.16777). **Implication for a probabilistic pipeline:** separate genuine signal from flakiness (quarantine/re-run known-flaky probes) *before* alerting, or real regressions hide in the noise.

- **Happy-path LLM evals are misleading.** High benchmark scores can be overfitting, not capability — guardrail models scored high on public sets but dropped sharply on novel prompts (*"Evaluating the Robustness of LLM Guardrails"*, arXiv:2511.22047, 2025, https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.22047). The standard antidote is adversarial/negative cases (e.g. HarmBench, Mazeika et al.).

**Direct application:** every safeguard the pipeline ships (velocity cap, doorway block, "is it live" check) must carry a **negative control in its self-test** — a fixture that the safeguard MUST reject. A `--test` that only feeds it a good input and sees PASS is the exact theatre this section warns against. (This is already the intent behind ZA's §584/§638/§651 "test that can fail" gates — the literature is the external evidence base.)

## Watchdog / measured-loop patterns

**Principle:** exit-code and HTTP-200 monitoring miss the most important failure — a job that *runs and reports success but produced nothing*. You need (a) a heartbeat/dead-man's-switch on the *scheduler*, and (b) a *freshness* check on the live *output*. Both, because the scheduler can fire successfully while the output stays stale/empty.

- **Dead-man's switch / heartbeat — alert on ABSENCE.** Dead Man's Snitch: "monitors for *absence*… guilty until proven innocent: assume the job failed unless it actively reports success." https://deadmanssnitch.com/docs/monitor/cron-jobs — Ping only on *successful completion that produced the intended result*; if the timer expires, it fires. **Healthchecks.io** (open-source, Python/Django): the job pings a unique URL on success; a missing ping → alert; a **Grace Time** absorbs expected jitter; detects machine-down, cron-not-running, non-zero exit, *and abnormally-long runs*. https://healthchecks.io/docs/monitoring_cron_jobs/ · source https://github.com/healthchecks/healthchecks. (Cronitor is a category peer — **[UNVERIFIED]**, docs not fetched.) **A daily pipeline that exits 0 but produced zero net-new live posts is the "vanished job" case** — only a result-gated heartbeat catches it.

- **Google SRE doctrine — symptoms, golden signals, error budgets.** *Monitoring Distributed Systems*, Google SRE Book, 2016: the **four golden signals** (latency, traffic, errors, saturation), distinguishing latency of *successful* vs *failed* requests. https://sre.google/sre-book/monitoring-distributed-systems/ — **Alert on symptoms, not causes**: "Alerts should be based on end-to-end measures of customer experience… If you alert on causes you risk being overwhelmed." https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/developers-practitioners/why-focus-symptoms-not-causes · *Practical Alerting* https://sre.google/sre-book/practical-alerting/ — **Every alert must be actionable**; a recurring alert nobody acts on (the "same quiet note forever") means the alert is mis-designed. **Burn-rate / multi-window alerting** escalates severity: a fast burn pages now, a slow burn opens a ticket, so a long stall gets *louder over time* rather than repeating one note. *Alerting on SLOs*, Google SRE Workbook. https://sre.google/workbook/alerting-on-slos/

- **Data freshness / staleness — the symptom for a content product.** Monte Carlo's five pillars of data observability: **freshness, distribution, volume, schema, lineage** — freshness = "how up-to-date your tables are and the cadence at which they update", and "data downtime" = periods where data is missing/erroneous. https://www.montecarlodata.com/blog-introducing-the-5-pillars-of-data-observability/ — **dbt source freshness** is a concrete three-state staleness gate: compare a `loaded_at` timestamp against `warn_after` (→WARN) and `error_after` (→ERROR, halt before building on stale data). https://docs.getdbt.com/reference/resource-properties/freshness — **Critical footgun:** "if neither threshold is provided, dbt will not calculate freshness" — i.e. an *unconfigured* freshness check silently never fires. (`great_expectations` is the standard data-validation peer — **[UNVERIFIED]**, docs not fetched. Airflow SLA-miss detection exists but the classic `sla` arg changed/deprecated in Airflow 2.9+ — **[UNVERIFIED]**, verify against current docs.)

**Direct application:** the measured-loop must measure **net-new LIVE posts** (live sitemap delta + HTTP 200), never `src/`/`page.tsx` count — a generated-but-stranded post must NOT read as "published." The alarm must **escalate by stall duration** and **de-duplicate** (not the same quiet note forever). This is precisely ZA's §621/§645 design; the SRE/MLOps sources above are the external evidence that the design is correct, and they add one fix: **alert on the freshness symptom the user feels (staleness), not on internal step success.**

## Concrete techniques to apply to a failing daily-pipeline (ranked, actionable)

Ranked by impact-per-effort against the four named symptoms. Each names **which symptom it kills** and the **source pattern**.

**1. Replace LLM/self-assessed "published" with an externally-graded acceptance criterion. — kills: silent daily failure, stranded posts.**
"Done" = the *live sitemap.xml gained N new URLs AND each returns HTTP 200 AND is non-doorway*, graded against the real world, never the agent's report or a source-tree file count. This is SWE-bench's `FAIL_TO_PASS` (goal achieved) + `PASS_TO_PASS` (no regression) split applied to publishing, and the direct antidote to AutoGPT's natural-language "done." (Sources: SWE-bench harness https://github.com/princeton-nlp/SWE-bench ; AutoGPT failure case-study; spec-driven "definition of done.") *ZA already has §621 measuring live sitemap delta — enforce it as the ONLY definition of published.*

**2. Give every safeguard a negative control in its self-test. — kills: "no matter what is programmed, failures recur."**
The reason programmed caps/blocks don't stick is that their tests can't fail — they verify the happy path only. Every velocity-cap and doorway-block `--test` must include a fixture it MUST reject (a 9th post on a day capped at 8 → must be blocked; a doorway page → must not publish). A safeguard whose test cannot fail is not a safeguard. (Sources: mutation testing / PIT; "Sanity Checks for Saliency Maps" negative controls; metamorphic relations.) *Aligns with ZA §584/§638/§651 — this is the literature backing.*

**3. Make the watchdog a result-gated dead-man's switch with duration-escalating, de-duplicated alerts. — kills: silent daily failure.**
Alert on the *absence* of net-new live posts (the symptom), not on exit code. Escalate by stall duration (quiet → loud → EMERGENCY at 2N), de-dupe to one note/day, and ensure each alert is *actionable*. Audit the freshness check itself for the dbt footgun: an unconfigured/path-mismatched threshold *silently never fires* (this is exactly the §645 path-contract bug — producer wrote where the checker never reads). (Sources: Dead Man's Snitch; Healthchecks.io; Google SRE symptom-alerting + burn-rate; dbt source freshness footgun.)

**4. Add a termination/progress-memory gate so the pipeline cannot regenerate posts it never landed. — kills: stranded posts, blown velocity caps.**
MAST attributes ~17% of multi-agent failures to step repetition from no awareness of prior progress; AutoGPT loops the same way. Before generating, the pipeline must read live state and skip topics already live; after generating, it must *land or loudly fail*, never silently strand. A stranded (generated-but-uncommitted) post is a first-class FAILURE state with its own alarm, not a no-op. (Sources: MAST arXiv:2503.13657; AutoGPT case study; LangGraph durable checkpoint+`thread_id` for cross-run state https://docs.langchain.com/oss/python/langgraph/interrupts.)

**5. Shorten the chain and verify each step's real effect; treat error compounding as the enemy. — kills: silent daily failure, blown caps.**
A long generate→sanitise→dash-normalise→commit→deploy→index chain multiplies per-step failure (99%/step → 36.6% over 100 steps). Make each step verify its *real* effect via the observation it produces (CodeAct/OpenHands pattern: act *as* executable code, read stdout/exit/state), and fail loudly at the step that breaks rather than passing a subtly-wrong artefact downstream. The known ZA insert-path dash-strand bug (title/excerpt bypassed §547 normalize → commit rejected → stranded every run) is exactly this class. (Sources: Six Sigma Agent arXiv:2601.22290 error-compounding maths; OpenHands/CodeAct arXiv:2407.16741; SWE-agent ACI tool-boundary validity https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.15793.)

**6. Move the velocity cap and doorway rule OUT of mid-prompt instruction into a deterministic code gate. — kills: blown caps, "failures recur."**
Lost-in-the-middle predicts a rule buried in a long prompt is applied least reliably. A cap or content-policy enforced as deterministic code at the chokepoint (fail-closed default) does not depend on the model "remembering" it. This is the chokepoint-fix + fail-safe-default discipline (ZA §627), and it is why spec-driven external contracts beat in-prompt rules. (Sources: Lost in the Middle arXiv:2307.03172; spec-driven development; Anthropic "Building Effective Agents" — keep rules in code paths, not buried prose.)

**7. Add an independent verifier/critic step that gates persistence — and only commit work that passed. — kills: stranded/low-quality posts.**
Voyager's ablation showed that *removing* the separate critic let buggy code accumulate in the library; the fix is a distinct verifier (separate model or, better, a non-LLM check) that must pass before an artefact is committed/published. Per the self-correction literature, make the verifier's signal *independent and ground-truth-grounded* — a test/critic-agent, not the generator re-reading itself. (Sources: Voyager arXiv:2305.16291; AutoGen critic agent; generator–verifier gap; Huang et al. on why intrinsic self-correction fails.)

**8. Feed the SPECIFIC failure back into a bounded retry — don't blind-retry. — kills: silent daily failure (improves recovery).**
When a step fails (commit rejected, 404, not-in-sitemap), construct a retry prompt/path that states *exactly* what was wrong, bounded by a budget, then escalate loudly if exhausted — never an unbounded blind loop. (Sources: DSPy `dspy.Assert` backtracking arXiv:2312.13382; Guardrails AI `OnFailAction=reask`; Instructor `max_retries` with error fed back; Reflexion reflection-into-memory.) *Aligns with ZA §606/§437 self-healing loops.*

**Why the succeeding projects succeed and this one doesn't:** the client-work projects have a human (Courtney) supplying ground-truth feedback every turn ("PR" / corrections) — an external, independent signal closing the loop, exactly what Reflexion/process-reward show is the active ingredient. The blog pipeline runs *unattended*, so it has been self-grading with no independent signal — the precise theatre condition (Huang et al.) under which programmed safeguards do not stick. The ranked actions above replace the missing human-feedback signal with *machine* ground-truth signals (live-sitemap grading, negative-control tests, result-gated watchdog, deterministic gates) so the unattended pipeline gets the same closed loop the attended projects get for free.
