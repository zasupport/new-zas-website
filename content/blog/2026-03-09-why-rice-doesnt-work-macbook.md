## The Rice Myth: Why It Feels True But Isn't

The rice method has been passed around since the early days of smartphone ownership, and it persists because it occasionally appears to work. Someone dunks their phone in water, buries it in rice overnight, turns it on the next morning, and it boots up fine. Rice saved it!

Except it didn't. The phone dried on its own. The rice was just sitting there.

Here's the reality: uncooked rice has a **relatively low desiccant capability** compared to purpose-made silica gel packets, and even those wouldn't be sufficient to address the real problem inside a water-damaged MacBook. The rice is sitting outside a sealed aluminium chassis, millimetres away from the components that actually matter. It is not pulling moisture off your logic board traces. It is not wicking water out from underneath your keyboard membrane, your battery connector, or your USB-C charging board.

Rice also introduces another hazard: fine starch dust particles can be drawn into your MacBook's ventilation system, adding contamination on top of an already compromised machine. What's more, while your MacBook sits in rice for 24 to 72 hours — time you feel you're being proactive — corrosion is actively progressing on your logic board.

Rice doesn't fix liquid damage. It delays your repair. And that delay costs you money.

## What Liquid Actually Does to a MacBook Logic Board

This is where understanding the real enemy matters.

When liquid — whether it's water, coffee, juice, or wine — enters your MacBook, it doesn't just make things wet. The moment that liquid contacts the energised circuits on your logic board, a process called **electrolytic corrosion** begins. Electricity passes through the liquid, which acts as a conductor, and this causes the metallic traces, solder joints, and component pads on the board to oxidise and degrade.

Even if your MacBook powers off immediately — or you power it off the second the spill happens — residual charge in the board's capacitors can still drive this corrosive process. The liquid doesn't need mains power to do damage. It brings its own chemistry.

What this looks like under a microscope:

- **Green or white oxidation** forming on copper traces and solder pads
- **Corroded vias** — the tiny drilled holes that connect circuit layers — blocking electrical pathways
- **Component failure** as the corrosion eats through resistors, capacitors, and power management chips
- **Short circuits** developing as oxidised material bridges components that should be isolated

Tap water contains dissolved minerals. Coffee contains acids. Juice contains sugars that leave conductive residue even after the water evaporates. This is why leaving a MacBook in rice for three days while the liquid "dries out" is so destructive — the water may evaporate, but the conductive and corrosive residue it leaves behind does not. In fact, as water evaporates it concentrates those minerals and acids, making the corrosive deposit worse, not better.

By the time you retrieve your MacBook from the rice, it may appear dry. But the board is covered in a layer of corrosive chemistry that is silently destroying your machine.

## The Corrosion Timeline: Why Every Hour Matters

We've been repairing liquid-damaged MacBooks for 14 years at ZA Support, and we can tell you with confidence: **the single biggest factor in repair success is how quickly the machine reaches us**.

Here's a rough timeline of what happens after a liquid spill:

- **0–2 hours:** Liquid is still mobile. Corrosion is beginning but minimal. Ultrasonic cleaning at this stage has excellent success rates.
- **2–12 hours:** Corrosion spreading. Some component damage possible. Repair still highly viable but success rate begins to decline.
- **12–48 hours:** Significant corrosion established. Multiple components may be affected. Logic board repair becomes more complex and costly.
- **48–72+ hours (the typical rice timeline):** Corrosion has had days to propagate across the board. Traces may be permanently damaged. Component replacement likelihood increases substantially.

This is not a scare tactic. This is the electrochemical reality of what liquid does to densely packed modern circuits. A MacBook Pro M3 logic board has components measured in nanometres sitting millimetres apart. Corrosion doesn't need much time or space to cause catastrophic damage.

If your MacBook gets wet, the right move is to power it off immediately, do not attempt to turn it back on, and get it to a specialist as fast as possible. Do not charge it. Do not test it. Do not put it in rice.

### Step 3: Ultrasonic Cleaning

This is the critical step that rice and home drying cannot replicate.

We submerge your logic board in our ultrasonic cleaning bath filled with isopropyl alcohol-based cleaning solution. The ultrasonic cleaner generates **high-frequency sound waves** (typically 40,000 Hz) that create millions of microscopic bubbles in the cleaning fluid through a process called **cavitation**. These bubbles implode against every surface of the board — including underneath components, inside connectors, and along trace edges — physically dislodging corrosive mineral deposits, oxidation, and conductive residue.

The result is a board cleaned at a microscopic level that no brush, swab, or aircan could reach.

After ultrasonic cleaning, the board is thoroughly rinsed, inspected under magnification, and dried in a controlled environment.

### Step 4: Inspection and Micro-Soldering

Post-cleaning, our technicians inspect the board under high-magnification for corrosion damage that has already compromised traces or components. Where necessary, we perform **micro-soldering repairs** — re-balling BGA chips, replacing corroded capacitors and resistors, bridging damaged traces with fine wire repair — using professional-grade equipment.

This is [logic board repair](/logic-board-repair) at its most detailed, and it's why experience matters. Our technicians have performed this work for 14 years.

### Step 5: Powered Testing

Once the board is clean and any micro-soldering is complete, we power test it methodically — checking all subsystems including power delivery, display output, keyboard, trackpad, USB-C, Wi-Fi, and storage — before reassembly.
