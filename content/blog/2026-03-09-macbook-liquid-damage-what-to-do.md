## Step One: Do These Things Immediately

The moment liquid contacts your MacBook, the clock starts. Every second the machine stays powered on while wet increases the risk of permanent damage. Here's your emergency checklist — do these in order, right now.

**1. Hold down the power button until the MacBook turns off.**
Don't close apps. Don't save your document. Don't do anything except hold that button until the screen goes dark. If it's already off, keep it off. Do not turn it back on to "check if it still works." This is the single biggest mistake people make and it causes catastrophic, irreversible damage to the logic board.

**2. Unplug everything.**
Power cable, USB hubs, external drives, headphones, dongles — all of it out. Right now.

**3. If it's a MacBook with a removable battery (older models, pre-2012), remove the battery immediately.**
Newer MacBooks have sealed batteries, so skip this step and don't attempt to open the machine yourself.

**4. Turn it upside down, like a tent.**
Open the lid to about 90 degrees and rest it upside down on a towel. This lets gravity help drain liquid away from the logic board rather than deeper into it. Leave it in this position.

**5. Wipe down the exterior.**
Use a dry, lint-free cloth to absorb any liquid you can see on the keyboard, screen, and casing. Be gentle. Don't shake the machine.

**6. Call ZA Support on 064 529 5863.**
Seriously. Call now, even if it's after hours. The sooner we know what happened, the better advice we can give you while you're on your way to us in Hyde Park.

## What NOT To Do After Liquid Damage

The internet is full of bad advice about water-damaged electronics. Here's what to avoid:

### Do Not Put It in Rice

We're going to say this clearly: **rice does not fix liquid-damaged MacBooks**. This myth has cost South Africans thousands of rands in repair bills that could have been avoided.

Here's why rice is useless — and actively harmful. Rice absorbs atmospheric moisture, not the liquid that has already coated your logic board. It does nothing to remove the corrosive residue left behind by water, coffee, juice, or cooldrink. Meanwhile, while your MacBook sits in a bag of rice for two days, the liquid that's already inside is oxidising your logic board, corroding solder joints, and destroying capacitors. By the time you bring it in, what would have been a R 2,500 repair has turned into a R 6,500 board-level repair — or worse, an irreplaceable board.

Rice delays treatment. That's all it does.

### Do Not Use a Hairdryer

Heat causes components to expand. It can warp the logic board, damage ribbon cables, and push moisture deeper into the chassis. Avoid any heat source entirely.

### Do Not Turn It Back On

We know. You want to know if it works. Please resist this urge. Powering on a wet MacBook is like turning on your car engine when you know oil has leaked into the cylinders. The damage isn't from the water — it's from electricity running through wet circuits. Keep it off.

### Do Not Wait a Week to "See If It Dries Out"

Liquid damage is a time-sensitive emergency. The longer corrosion is allowed to develop on your MacBook's logic board, the more components will fail. A MacBook brought in within 24–48 hours has a significantly higher chance of a clean, cost-effective repair than one brought in a week later.

## What's Actually Happening Inside Your MacBook Right Now

Understanding the science helps explain why speed matters so much.

When liquid enters your MacBook, it doesn't just sit there. The moment water — or anything water-based — contacts the metal traces on your logic board, an electrochemical reaction begins. If the board is powered (or was powered when the spill happened), this process accelerates dramatically.

The liquid creates conductive bridges between circuits that shouldn't be connected, causing short circuits. These short circuits generate heat, which can destroy MOSFETs, capacitors, and integrated circuits in seconds. Even after you power off, the residue left behind continues to corrode metal traces, eating through copper pathways like rust eats through metal left in the rain.

Coffee, juice, and cooldrink are particularly destructive because they contain sugars and acids that accelerate corrosion and leave a sticky, conductive residue even after they "dry out." Water from the tap isn't much better — it contains minerals that leave conductive deposits.

This is why professional ultrasonic cleaning of the logic board is so important. It's not just about drying the machine out. It's about removing every trace of residue before corrosion can take hold.
